“There Was No Choice but to be Pioneers”: Women in Software Exhibit

This exhibit focuses on the theme of women in software and big tech companies. As software is a newer field, eras are all relatively recent and jumbled together. Our exhibit begins by examining the precursors to software, such as the earliest computing technology and machines. We witness the evolution of technology to today in our everyday lives, through use of computers, smartphones, AI, etc. The beginning of the era of software is defined by Ada Lovelace, known as the first computer programmer. Key moments throughout its history include basic computing and calculation through weather apps and calculators, and then the rise of the big tech industry with well-known companies such as Microsoft and Google. Our exhibit discusses trailblazers like Grace Hopper, Margaret Hamilton, Ginni Rometty, and Nancy Rhine.

Historically, due to immigration, the supply of workers has increased while also creating demand for marketing and communication devices. There was initially a rise in women’s involvement in the field during the second World War with a push for technology, and then a recession that followed. This is still the case today, with women continuing to face discrimination in the tech industry through unjust hirings, wage gaps, and minimal opportunity for growth.

In the call for a national women’s history museum, it is critical that our exhibit is included as technology and software are as important as ever in the modern world, and our future will only become more digitalized. Our exhibit outlines the American female experience through a comprehensive timeline, specific artifacts, and modern perspectives from women at Microsoft. It is important for students and the public alike to learn about women’s contributions in the field as not only is the historical perspective otherwise incomplete, but women made the most substantial contributions in the field as well. After women made up the largest portion of the workforce in software during the second World War, they were slowly (and purposefully) replaced by men.

Today, only twenty-two percent of computer scientists are women. Our exhibit proves that it does not need to be a male-dominated field and increasing women’s participation will start with education through looking at the history. Why is it that the number of women working in software is practically at an all-time low? How do we change this? For further information, see artifacts, timeline, and interviews.

In forming this exhibit, we were still forced to leave out certain perspectives and people because the history is simply too extensive. Although Ada Lovelace is included, in focusing primarily on American women in software, the global perspective is inherently weakened. Although focusing on one topic allows us to get more specific, the history is left incomplete without international women’s contributions to software. Since only so many important figures could be included, others had to be left out. For example, in telling the story of NASA’s moon launch, the stories of women like JoAnn Morgan, Katherine Johnson, and Judy Sullivan were left out. We encourage visitors to look over our works cited to facilitate finding further information.
