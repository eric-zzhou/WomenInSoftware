Ada Lovelace (1815 - 1852)

Lovelace and Babbage’s Analytical Machine

Ada Lovelace was born in 1815 in London. With her parents separating early in her life, she was primarily self-educated, receiving some help from private tutors and then Augustus De Morgan, the professor of math at the University of London. She is well-known as the first computer programmer due to her work on Charles Babbage’s Difference Machine, a large box of wheels and gears that could perform calculations when a handle was pulled. Lovelace helped turn this simple calculator of the Difference Machine into the Analytical Machine, involving even more calculations and introducing algorithms to its calculations.

“Imagination is the Discovering Faculty,

pre-eminently. It is that which penetrates

into the unseen worlds around us, the

worlds of Science" -Ada Lovelace


When mathematician Luigi Federico wrote an eight-thousand-word paper on the subject, Lovelace not only translated it into English but added notes that were three times the length of the original. These notes detailed how it would weave complex algebraic operations, similar to a silk loom, and how it would compute the Bernoulli numbers (the series of positive rational numbers). She was also the first person to separate the science of computing from math. As she described it, "The science of operations, as derived from mathematics more especially, is a science of itself, and has its own abstract truth and value."


Lovelace also explained the significance of the machine itself in the field of computing and for the future, such as how this new language of innovation will go on to illuminate the natural world to humankind. Her notes were so prophetic that scholars would argue that her role was greatly overexaggerated, or that the notes were not written by her. This coincided with the period when computing was being reclaimed as a male activity during the 1900s. For her revolutionary work in computing, Lovelace is known as the first computer programmer, with the language Ada developed in her honor by the Department of Defense.
